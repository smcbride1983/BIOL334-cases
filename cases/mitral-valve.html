Case Study

🫀 Patient Case: Dental-Associated Infective Endocarditis

Patient Background

Ricarda Wright, a 63-year-old woman with a history of rheumatic heart disease, presents to the hospital with fatigue, low-grade fever, and joint pain that have gradually worsened over the past month. Three weeks ago, she underwent a tooth extraction for a severe dental abscess. Since then, she has noticed increasing weakness, poor appetite, and unintentional weight loss.

Clinical Findings

  • Temperature: 100.9 °F
  • Heart rate: 102 bpm
  • Blood pressure: 116/70 mm Hg
  • Cardiac exam: new systolic murmur heard best at the apex
  • Mild splenomegaly noted on abdominal examination
  • Weight loss of 8 pounds over the past month
  • Echocardiogram: vegetations present on the mitral valve
  • Blood work: WBC 14,200/µL, elevated ESR and CRP
Sample Collected: Blood cultures

Hospital Concern

The cardiology team suspects infective endocarditis involving a previously damaged native mitral valve. The recent dental procedure raises concern that bacteria from the oral microbiota entered the bloodstream and colonized the heart valve.

Lab Task

  1. Perform a Gram stain on the blood culture to determine Gram reaction, shape, and arrangement.
  2. Inoculate appropriate media and perform biochemical tests to identify the pathogen.
  3. After identifying the organism, set up a Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion test on Mueller-Hinton agar.
  4. Report the most likely organism and recommend an antibiotic that may be effective.
