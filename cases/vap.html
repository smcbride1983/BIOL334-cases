Case 6: Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

BIOL 334 Clinical Microbiology Case Study

Patient Background

A 64-year-old man was admitted to the ICU after a severe motor vehicle accident. He required intubation and mechanical ventilation. On hospital day 7, he develops a new fever, increased oxygen requirement, and thick purulent respiratory secretions.

A chest radiograph shows a new right lower-lobe infiltrate. The ICU team collects an endotracheal aspirate and sends it to the microbiology lab for culture and identification.

Initial Clinical Data

  • Temperature: 39.1°C
  • WBC count: 18,400 cells/µL
  • Respiratory secretions: Thick, greenish, purulent sputum
  • Chest X-ray: New right lower-lobe infiltrate
  • Specimen: Endotracheal aspirate

Gram Stain

The Gram stain from the respiratory specimen shows many neutrophils and numerous Gram-negative rods.

Culture Results

After incubation, the organism grows well on blood agar and MacConkey agar. Colonies are large, flat, and spreading with a faint greenish pigment and a grape-like odor.

Clinical Explanation

This patient likely has ventilator-associated pneumonia, a hospital-acquired infection that develops after prolonged mechanical ventilation. The clinical setting, Gram-negative rods, growth on MacConkey agar, oxidase positivity, non-fermenting metabolism, pigment production, and characteristic odor are all important clues.
